Blackwell: tcgen05 & tensor memory B200

The first time I moved a working Hopper GEMM onto a B200 I expected a tune-up and got a rewrite. For four generations the tensor-core deal had been stable: you fed it operands out of registers or shared memory, it did the matrix-multiply-accumulate, and the result came back to your registers. Everything I had learned climbing the GEMM ladder — tiling, double-buffering, wgmma on Hopper — assumed that the accumulator lived where my threads live. Blackwell breaks that assumption. The 5th-generation tensor core, driven by the tcgen05 instruction family, does its accumulation in a brand-new physical memory space that your threads cannot address as ordinary variables, and it expects two thread blocks to feed a single MMA. This is not a bigger wgmma. It is a different machine, and I learned the hard way that porting a Hopper kernel to it naively leaves most of the chip on the floor.

This article is the map of that new machine: what tcgen05 is, what Tensor Memory (TMEM) is and why it exists, how CTA pairs cooperate on one instruction, and — the part that actually costs you a week — why the whole kernel has to be restructured around it. Everything below is B200 / GB300 (sm_100a), the Blackwell data-center parts.1 "Blackwell" covers a range of dies. The GB300 / B200 data-center chips are sm_100; the consumer RTX 50-series is a different sm_120 variant with the same programming model but different resource sizes. Everything here targets sm_100a — the a suffix, as on Hopper's sm_90a, means "architecture-specific, no forward-compat guarantee".

The problem Blackwell is solving

Recall the ridge point from the three regimes: to stay compute-bound your kernel must do enough FLOPs per byte to keep the math units fed. Each generation adds tensor-core throughput faster than it adds bandwidth and faster than it adds register-file capacity, and that second squeeze is the one that produced tcgen05.

On Hopper, the tensor core reads its operands from shared memory (via wgmma's descriptor form) but writes the accumulator into the register file — 256 KB per SM, 65536 32-bit registers, capped at 255 per thread. A large BF16 accumulator tile is expensive in that budget. A 128 x 256 FP32 accumulator is 32768 floats; spread across a warpgroup of 128 threads that is 256 registers per thread — over the limit before you have counted a single operand or loop variable. The register file became the wall. You could make the tensor cores faster, but you had nowhere to put the answers.

Blackwell's fix is structural: give the accumulator its own memory.

Tensor Memory (TMEM)

Tensor Memory is a dedicated 256 KB per-SM scratchpad whose only job is to hold tensor-core operands and accumulators (256 KB = 128 lanes × 512 columns × 4 bytes). It is addressed as a 2-D grid — 128 lanes by 512 columns of 32 bits — physically separate from the 256 KB SMEM+L1 block and from the 256 KB register file, so do not add these up expecting one pool; they are three distinct SRAM structures on the SM. That geometry is not arbitrary — it mirrors the shape the tcgen05 MMA wants to write, so the tensor core can stream results into TMEM at full rate without a transpose.

The crucial property, the one that reorganizes your entire kernel, is this: your threads cannot treat TMEM as ordinary memory. There is no pointer you dereference, no float acc = tmem[i]. TMEM is written only by the tensor core, and read back into registers only through explicit copy instructions:

// You do not "declare" a TMEM accumulator; you allocate a region of it.
uint32_t tmem_addr;                       // an opaque lane:col handle, not a pointer
tcgen05.alloc.b32   [&tmem_addr], nCols;  // reserve nCols columns of TMEM

// The MMA accumulates INTO that region, reading operands from SMEM:
tcgen05.mma.cta_group::2.kind::f16
        [tmem_addr], descA, descB, idesc, /*accumulate=*/1;

// To touch the result at all, you must copy it out to registers:
tcgen05.ld.sync.aligned.32x32b.x128.b32  {r0, r1, ...}, [tmem_addr];

tcgen05.dealloc.b32 tmem_addr, nCols;     // and you MUST free it

Three things in that sketch are worth staring at. First, tmem_addr is an opaque handle — a packed lane/column coordinate the allocator hands you, not a byte address you can do arithmetic on. Second, the accumulator is never in registers during the MMA loop, so the register pressure that capped Hopper accumulators simply disappears; your 255 registers are now free for operands, indices, and pipelining state. Third, tcgen05.alloc and tcgen05.dealloc are real allocation calls against a tiny fixed pool — forget the dealloc and the next kernel that needs TMEM stalls or fails.2 TMEM allocation is coarse and column-granular, and the pool is per-SM. Allocation is done by a single warp on behalf of the block. In practice you allocate your accumulator region once at kernel start and free it once at the end — treating it like malloc/free inside the inner loop is a good way to serialize your SM.

tcgen05: a single-instruction warp… of one thread

The programming model of the MMA itself is the second surprise. Hopper's wgmma was a warpgroup instruction: all 128 threads of four warps issued it collectively, each contributing registers. tcgen05.mma is issued by a single thread. One thread points the tensor core at an operand descriptor for A, a descriptor for B, and a TMEM accumulator handle, and the hardware does the entire tile.

This sounds like a simplification, and at the issue site it is — but it moves all the difficulty into feeding the beast. Because one instruction now consumes enormous tiles, the operands are almost always in shared memory, described to the tensor core by a 64-bit matrix descriptor encoding the SMEM base address, the leading dimension, and the swizzle pattern. The tensor core walks SMEM itself. Your threads' job shrinks to: get the right bytes into SMEM in the right swizzled layout, before the descriptor points at them, without stalling. That is the whole game now, and it is why the async copy engine matters so much on Blackwell.

CTA pairs: two blocks, one MMA

The third change is the one with no Hopper analogue. Blackwell can pair two adjacent SMs — technically two CTAs (Cooperative Thread Arrays, i.e. thread blocks) in the same cluster — so that they cooperate on a single MMA. You saw the syntax above: tcgen05.mma.cta_group::2. That cta_group::2 says "this MMA spans a CTA pair."

The motivation is the same register/TMEM economics scaled up. The largest, most efficient tcgen05 shapes are wider than one SM's TMEM and SMEM can comfortably supply. So Blackwell lets the pair split the work: one CTA (the "leader") holds and accumulates the shared portion of the tile in its TMEM, while both CTAs stage their halves of the A/B operands into their own shared memories, reachable across the pair through the cluster's distributed shared memory (DSMEM) fabric that Hopper introduced.3 The CTA-pair mechanism reuses Hopper's thread-block-cluster and DSMEM machinery — the two SMs must be in the same cluster so the shared-memory window is mutually addressable. Blackwell adds the tensor-core-level cooperation on top; you still launch with a cluster dimension the way you would for a sm_90a clustered kernel. Effectively, two SMs' worth of SMEM bandwidth and TMEM capacity back one logical tensor-core operation, which is how Blackwell keeps the 5th-gen core fed at shapes that would starve a lone SM.

For the kernel author this means the tiling you learned for a single SM is now a tiling across a pair of SMs. Your block-tile is co-owned. The leader/follower asymmetry — who allocates the accumulator, who issues the MMA, who drains TMEM — is something you now design explicitly, and getting it wrong shows up as one SM in the pair sitting idle.

Why the kernel gets restructured

Put the three changes together and a Hopper GEMM does not "port" to Blackwell; it inverts. On Hopper the mental model was threads own the accumulator and grind the K-loop. On Blackwell the model is threads are a memory-movement crew servicing an autonomous tensor core, and the kernel is a pipeline with three roles running concurrently:

  1. Producer — a warp (often a specialized "DMA warp") that issues TMA async copies to keep the next A/B tiles landing in SMEM, several stages ahead.
  2. MMA issuer — the single thread firing tcgen05.mma in a loop, each iteration accumulating one K-step into the same TMEM region, gated on the producer's tiles being ready.
  3. Epilogue / consumer — warps that, once the K-loop finishes, run tcgen05.ld to pull the finished accumulator out of TMEM, apply scales/activation, and write C.

These are decoupled by asynchronous barriers (mbarrier) rather than by the lockstep of a warpgroup. The reason this restructuring is mandatory, not optional, is that the tensor core is now fast enough that any bubble — a late SMEM tile, an early TMEM read that races the accumulation — idles a unit that represents a huge fraction of the chip's 989-class TFLOP/s of throughput on a data-center Blackwell part.4 I am deliberately not quoting a single peak-TFLOP number for B200 the way we quote 989 TFLOP/s BF16 for H100 — Blackwell's headline figures are usually cited at FP4 with sparsity, which is not the regime you actually hit. The honest statement is: per-SM dense tensor throughput is a large multiple of Hopper's, and the whole tcgen05/TMEM design exists so software can reach it.

Where NVFP4 comes in

The other reason to restructure is precision. Blackwell's tensor cores natively support NVFP4 — a 4-bit float in e2m1 form (1 sign, 2 exponent, 1 mantissa bit) paired with per-block FP8 (e4m3) scale factors.5 NVFP4 is microscaled: a block of 16 e2m1 values shares one e4m3 scale, and there is typically a second, coarser per-tensor FP32 scale on top. The 4-bit values carry almost no dynamic range on their own; the block scales are what make the format usable, and staging them correctly alongside the operands is a real part of the kernel's SMEM budget. Four-bit operands mean you move a quarter of the bytes of BF16 for the same tile — which, per the ridge-point argument, is exactly how you keep a tensor core this fast on the compute side of the wall. But it means every operand tile now arrives with a companion scale tile, and the epilogue must apply scales as it drains TMEM. The public Blackwell NVFP4 kernel worklogs bear this out: the reported climb — a batched NVFP4 GEMM going from a naive ~2000 μs down to roughly 22 μs — is almost entirely a story of coalescing the FP4 loads, converting with intrinsics like __nv_cvt_fp4x2_to_halfraw2, and packing scales so the tensor core never waits. The MMA was never the bottleneck. Feeding it was.

The through-line

Zoom out and Blackwell is the same lesson the whole ladder has been teaching, taken to its limit. Kernel 1 was memory-bound because every thread re-read HBM. Blackwell is memory-bound in a subtler way: the compute unit got so fast that the entire kernel — SMEM staging, TMEM allocation, CTA-pair coordination, scale handling — exists to prevent a single bubble in front of it. tcgen05 moved the accumulator out of your registers and into TMEM; the CTA pair pooled two SMs behind one instruction; and in doing so it turned the kernel author from someone who computes into someone who choreographs. The next article puts a profiler on one of these pipelines and asks the same question we have asked since the beginning — what is it waiting on? — because on Blackwell the answer is almost never the math.
