01 · The GPU, From Silicon Up

The Streaming Multiprocessor SM

If you flatten an H100 down to the one part that actually runs your code, you get the Streaming Multiprocessor (SM). Everything above it — the 8 Graphics Processing Clusters (GPCs), the crossbar, the memory controllers — exists to keep the SMs fed. Everything below it is silicon that only moves when an SM tells it to. When you write a CUDA kernel, you are not really programming "a GPU"; you are describing the work of one thread block, and the machine's whole job is to pack thousands of those blocks onto ~132 SMs and run them until the answer falls out. So this is where we start the course, and it is the box we will keep coming back to for the rest of it: every occupancy decision, every latency-hiding trick, every register-versus-shared-memory tradeoff in the entire GEMM ladder is, in the end, a decision about what fits inside one SM.

The honest scope note: I am going to describe the SM as it matters to a kernel writer, not as a chip architect. That means I care about the four sub-partitions, the register file, the scheduler, and the shared-memory/L1 pool — the resources you can actually run out of — and I will wave at the rest.

One SM, four sub-partitions

The first surprise, if you picture an SM as a monolithic core, is that it isn't one. A Hopper SM is split into four sub-partitions (NVIDIA's diagrams call them processing blocks), and they are near-identical quarters of the SM. Each sub-partition is a small, self-contained execution engine, and understanding this quartering explains almost every "why is my occupancy weird" question you will ever ask.

Each of the four sub-partitions owns:

  • One warp scheduler and one dispatch unit. A warp is 32 threads that execute in lockstep. The scheduler's entire job, every clock, is to look at the warps assigned to its sub-partition, find one whose next instruction has its operands ready, and dispatch it. One instruction, for one warp, per cycle.1 More precisely: the scheduler can issue one warp instruction per cycle, but the functional unit it targets may take many cycles to retire it. Issue and completion are decoupled — that decoupling is exactly what latency hiding exploits.
  • A slice of the register file — the private scratchpad for the threads resident on that sub-partition.
  • 32 FP32 CUDA cores, 16 INT32 cores, 16 FP64 cores, plus load/store units (LSUs) that talk to memory and a Special Function Unit (SFU) for transcendentals like sin, rsqrt, exp.
  • One fourth-generation Tensor Core, the matrix-multiply unit that does the heavy lifting for deep learning and the thing the back half of this course is really about.

Multiply by four and you get the per-SM totals: 128 FP32 cores, 64 INT32, 64 FP64, and 4 Tensor Cores, fed by four schedulers. Because a warp is 32 threads wide and a sub-partition has exactly 32 FP32 lanes, an FP32 warp instruction maps one-thread-to-one-lane and clears in a single pass — which is why FP32 throughput is the "natural" width of the machine and INT32 or FP64, at 16 lanes, take two.

What every sub-partition keeps private, and what they share

Draw a line down the middle of the SM's resources and you split them into two piles, and this split is the whole game.

On the private side sits the register file. There are 65536 32-bit registers per SM — 256 KB, the single largest pool of state on the chip — and it is carved up among the four sub-partitions, then handed out to threads. This is the fastest storage the GPU has, effectively part of the datapath, and it is where your float acc accumulators and loop indices live. The catch is that it is finite in a way you feel constantly. A thread may use at most 255 registers2 255 is the hard ceiling the ISA can encode. Long before you hit it, nvcc will spill excess registers to local memory — which is really L1/L2/HBM wearing a costume — and a spill in your inner loop can quietly erase a 2× optimization., and the moment your kernel asks for more registers per thread, fewer threads fit, and occupancy drops. Every kernel in the GEMM ladder that gets faster by holding a tile of C in registers is spending exactly this budget.

On the shared side sits the combined shared memory / L1 cache pool: 256 KiB per SM, of which up to 228 KiB can be configured as programmer-managed shared memory (SMEM), with the remainder acting as a hardware-managed L1.3 That 228 KiB is a per-SM budget you split at launch, and the numbers are not perfectly round — a slice is reserved for the driver, so the usable figure is a little under the headline. Opting in above 48 KiB per block also requires an explicit cudaFuncSetAttribute call; the compiler will not hand it to you silently. All four sub-partitions read and write this same pool, which is what makes it the right place to stage data that a whole block of threads needs to share — a tile of A and B that everyone reuses. It is organized into 32 banks so that a full warp can hit it in one transaction when the addresses don't collide; when they do, you get bank conflicts, and much of the later ladder is about arranging your shared-memory layout so 32 lanes touch 32 different banks.

The design tension is now visible in one picture: registers are private and enormous but pressure occupancy; shared memory is communal and fast but tiny and easy to over-allocate. Nearly every kernel optimization is a negotiation between these two piles on one SM.

How a block lands on an SM

You launch a grid of thread blocks; the hardware's block scheduler (the GigaThread engine) hands blocks out to SMs as they have room. The word "room" is doing real work there. A block is placed on an SM only if the SM can satisfy all of the block's resource demands at once: enough registers for every thread, enough shared memory for the block's allocation, a free block slot, and enough warp slots. An H100 SM can hold up to 2048 resident threads — 64 warps — spread across its four sub-partitions, and a block can be at most 1024 threads.

Crucially, once a block is placed, it stays on that SM until every one of its warps finishes. There is no migration, no preemption mid-block in the normal case. The block's warps are parceled out across the four sub-partitions, its registers and shared memory are reserved for its entire lifetime, and only when it retires are those resources freed for the next block waiting in line. This is why a single over-hungry resource caps everything: ask for 96 KiB of shared memory per block and you will fit at most two blocks per SM no matter how few registers you use, because the third block cannot find shared memory to live in.

Why the SM is where latency goes to die

Here is the part that makes the whole design click, and it is the reason GPUs look nothing like CPUs. A CPU fights memory latency with giant caches, out-of-order execution, and branch prediction — expensive machinery to keep one thread moving. An SM does none of that. Its warp schedulers are dumb and cheap on purpose. The SM fights latency a completely different way: by having so many warps in flight that it never has to wait.

Walk through one cycle. A warp issues a global-memory load; that load will take hundreds of cycles to come back from HBM. On a CPU this would be a stall. On an SM, the scheduler simply marks that warp not ready and, on the very next cycle, dispatches an instruction from a different warp whose operands are already sitting in registers. With up to 64 warps resident and single-cycle context switching4 The "context switch" is nearly free precisely because it isn't a switch in the CPU sense — every resident warp's registers are already allocated in the register file. Nothing is saved or restored; the scheduler just changes which warp it reads from. That is the entire point of pinning registers for the block's lifetime., there is almost always someone ready to run. The memory latency is still hundreds of cycles, but it is hidden behind the useful work of other warps.

This is what occupancy — the ratio of resident warps to the SM's maximum of 64 — actually buys you: not raw parallelism, but latency-hiding slack. And it is why occupancy is a means, not an end. You need enough warps to cover your longest-latency operations, and past that point, more warps do nothing — while the registers and shared memory you spent to get them might have been better spent giving each thread more work to do. The entire art of the later kernels is finding that balance: exactly on this box, with exactly these four schedulers arbitrating exactly this register file.

That resolves a tension you may have already felt reading the three regimes. Whether a kernel is compute-bound or memory-bound is decided by arithmetic intensity — but whether the SM can hide the memory traffic it does have is decided right here, by whether there are enough warps resident to paper over the latency. A memory-bound kernel with high occupancy at least keeps its LSUs saturated; the same kernel starved of warps stalls dead. Regime tells you the ceiling; the SM tells you whether you'll reach it.

The bridge

So the mental model to carry forward is small and concrete. An SM is four sub-partitions, each with its own scheduler, dispatch, register slice, and CUDA/Tensor cores, all sharing one 256 KiB SMEM/L1 pool and one big register file. Blocks are admitted whole, pinned for their lifetime, and their warps are handed to the four schedulers, which hide latency by switching among however many warps you managed to fit. Every number in that sentence — 4, 64, 228, 255 — is a wall you will hit deliberately as we optimize.

We now have the unit of compute. Next we zoom out one level to see how ~132 of these SMs are wired together through the L2 cache and HBM3 into a full H100, and then we come straight back down here to write our first kernel — the naive GEMM that reaches a humiliating 1.3% of cuBLAS — and start reading, from the profiler, exactly which of this SM's resources it is wasting.
