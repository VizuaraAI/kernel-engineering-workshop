01 · The GPU, From Silicon Up

The L2 cache & partitions L2

Every byte that moves between HBM and a Streaming Multiprocessor (SM) passes through one shared checkpoint on the way, and almost nobody thinks about it until a profile forces them to. That checkpoint is the L2 cache — the last level of on-chip memory before you fall off the die into the 3.35 TB/s HBM3 pool. It is the one memory structure on the H100 that every SM touches, the place where atomics are settled, where compression happens, and where the difference between "my working set fits" and "my working set spills" is decided. This article is a tour of that checkpoint: how big it is, why it is split in two, what a "line" and a "sector" actually are, and the two knobs — residency and fetch granularity — that let you steer it.

I put this early in the course, right after the register file and shared memory, because L2 is the level people skip. Registers and shared memory are yours to command; global memory you learn to fear. L2 sits quietly between them, and it is the reason two kernels with identical arithmetic can differ 2× in wall-clock — one keeps its data resident, the other keeps re-fetching from HBM.

One cache for the whole die

Registers are private to a thread. Shared memory is private to a thread block, physically living inside one SM. The L2 is the opposite of both: it is a single logical cache shared across all ~132 SMs on the chip, sitting on the HBM side of the memory system. On an H100 SXM5 it is about 50 MiB of SRAM.1 The exact figure quoted by NVIDIA for the full GH100 is 50 MiB; shipping H100 SKUs with a few disabled partitions land slightly under. Treat "≈50 MiB" as the working number and never budget a kernel to the last kilobyte of it. That sounds small next to 80 GB of HBM, but relative to the on-chip world it is enormous — it dwarfs the combined 256 KiB of SMEM+L1 on any single SM by more than two orders of magnitude.

Because it is shared, the L2 is where cross-SM effects show up. If SM 7 writes a value and SM 90 reads it, they meet in L2. If a thousand threads across forty SMs do atomicAdd to the same counter, they serialize in L2, not in HBM. And if your total working set — the union of every tile every SM is chewing on — fits inside 50 MiB, you can be reading from HBM once and then living on-chip; if it doesn't, you thrash.

Two partitions and a crossbar

Here is the first thing that surprises people, and it is confirmed the moment you read anyone's honest H100 GEMM worklog: the L2 is not one monolithic block. It is physically split into two partitions, and each SM connects directly to one of them and reaches the other indirectly through a crossbar that bridges the two halves. The consequence is a near/far latency asymmetry — a hit in your local partition is cheaper than a hit that has to hop the crossbar to the other partition.2 This is why L2 hit latency is often quoted as a range rather than a single number. A "near" hit and a "far" hit are both L2 hits, but the far one pays the crossbar traversal. The variance is real and shows up in Nsight Compute's L2 latency histograms.

You can think of it as two ~25 MiB caches wearing one 50 MiB coat. NVIDIA's addressing tries to distribute lines so that, on average, traffic is balanced across both partitions and the crossbar stays busy but not saturated. For most kernels you never model this explicitly — you cannot pin a tile to "your" partition from CUDA C. But it explains observed behavior: two kernels with the same L2 hit rate can have different effective L2 latency depending on how their access pattern lands relative to the partitions, and it is one more reason that measured latency beats theoretical latency every single time.

The crossbar also matters for the thing L2 is quietly responsible for: global atomics. When you issue an atomicAdd to global memory, the read-modify-write does not round-trip to HBM. It is resolved in the L2, at the line that owns that address. That is why atomics on a hot address are "only" a few hundred cycles instead of a thousand-plus — and also why a contended atomic serializes: every contender is funneling into the same L2 line, possibly across the crossbar, one at a time.

Lines and sectors: the 128 B / 4 × 32 B granularity

Now zoom into the L2 itself. The cache is organized in cache lines of 128 bytes, and — this is the detail that governs every coalescing decision you will ever make — each 128 B line is divided into four 32-byte sectors. The sector, not the line, is the unit the memory system actually tracks for fetching and for hit/miss accounting.3 In Nsight Compute this is exactly why you see "sectors per request" as a first-class metric. A perfectly coalesced 32-thread warp reading floats touches all four sectors of one or more lines cleanly; a strided warp touches one sector out of every line it hits and drags 3 dead sectors of traffic behind each useful one.

Why does this matter? Because when a warp of 32 threads issues a load, the hardware coalesces those 32 addresses into the minimum set of sectors that cover them, and pays for whole sectors. If your 32 threads each read one float (4 bytes) contiguously, that is 128 bytes — one full line, all four sectors used, 100% efficiency. If your 32 threads are strided so each lands in a different line, you touch 32 sectors (1 KiB of traffic) to move the same 128 useful bytes, and you have just wasted 7/8 of your L2-to-SM bandwidth. This is the sector-level version of the coalescing story we hit in kernel 2 of the GEMM ladder — the reason a one-line change to how m and n map to threads took us from 1.3% to 8.5% of cuBLAS. That win was, mechanically, a sector-efficiency win.

Compression and the free lunch you didn't ask for

The H100's L2 carries data-compression circuitry — hardware that can compress certain data patterns on the fly as they sit in the cache and travel to and from HBM. The classic beneficiary is sparsity or repetition: buffers full of zeros, or activations after a ReLU, compress well, and the compressed representation moves fewer physical bytes across the HBM boundary. When it fires, you get effective bandwidth above the nominal 3.35 TB/s for free, because you are moving compressed bytes over the wire and expanding them on-chip.

The honest caveat: you do not control this from a kernel, and it is data-dependent, so never design a kernel assuming compression will save you. Treat it as an occasional tailwind — a gap Nsight shows between DRAM-bytes-read and the logical bytes you requested — not a lever. In the FP32 GEMM ladder it never mattered, because those kernels moved dense, high-entropy float tiles; compression pays off in memory-bound, low-entropy workloads, a different regime entirely (the three regimes is the article to reread).

Residency control: making the L2 keep your hot data

This is the lever that actually earns its place in a worklog. By default the L2 is a plain LRU-ish cache: it does not know that your 8 MiB embedding table is going to be read a million times while a one-shot 40 MiB streaming buffer flows through once. Left alone, the streaming buffer will evict your precious table, and you will pay HBM latency for data that "should" have stayed on-chip.

Hopper (and Ampere before it) lets you fix this with L2 residency control. You carve out a portion of the L2 as a persisting window and mark a specific address range as high-priority, so the cache preferentially keeps it resident and evicts the streaming traffic instead. The shape of it in CUDA is a cudaStreamAttrValue with an accessPolicyWindow — you hand it a base pointer, a number of bytes, and a hitRatio/hitProp that says "treat accesses in this window as persisting."

// Reserve up to a slice of L2 for persisting accesses, then
// mark [data, data + num_bytes) as high-priority ("persisting").
size_t persist_bytes = min(prop.persistingL2CacheMaxSize, (size_t)8 * 1024 * 1024);
cudaDeviceSetLimit(cudaLimitPersistingL2CacheSize, persist_bytes);

cudaStreamAttrValue attr = {};
attr.accessPolicyWindow.base_ptr  = reinterpret_cast<void*>(hot_data);
attr.accessPolicyWindow.num_bytes = persist_bytes;
attr.accessPolicyWindow.hitRatio  = 1.0f;                 // all of it, if it fits
attr.accessPolicyWindow.hitProp   = cudaAccessPropertyPersisting;
attr.accessPolicyWindow.missProp  = cudaAccessPropertyStreaming;
cudaStreamSetAttribute(stream, cudaStreamAttributeAccessPolicyWindow, &attr);

The missProp = cudaAccessPropertyStreaming half is as important as the persisting half: it tells the cache that everything outside the window is transient, so it should be evicted first. You are not just protecting the hot table; you are telling the L2 which traffic is disposable.4 There is a subtle failure mode: if num_bytes exceeds what you reserved with persistingL2CacheMaxSize, or if two streams both claim persisting windows that overlap in reserved capacity, you get thrashing that is worse than the default policy. Always read back prop.persistingL2CacheMaxSize and set hitRatio so the persisting footprint fits inside it. Used well, this turns a memory-bound kernel with a reused working set into something close to on-chip-resident. Used carelessly, it steals L2 capacity from everyone else and slows the whole launch down.

Fetch granularity: 32, 64, or 128 bytes

The last knob is subtle and workload-specific: the maximum L2 fetch granularity. When the L2 misses and has to pull from HBM, it fetches in a quantum — and you can cap that quantum with cudaDeviceSetLimit(cudaLimitMaxL2FetchGranularity, ...), choosing an upper bound in the neighborhood of 32, 64, or 128 bytes.

The trade-off is pure prefetch economics. A large granularity (128 B) is a bet on spatial locality: "if you needed one sector, you'll want its neighbors, so grab the whole line." For a dense GEMM streaming contiguous tiles that bet always pays — big fetches amortize the miss and keep HBM in its efficient burst regime. A small granularity (32 B) is the opposite bet, for sparse or scattered access — gather kernels, pointer-chasing, sparse-matrix formats — where fetching a full 128 B line to use 32 B of it just burns three sectors of HBM bandwidth on data you will never read.5 This limit is an upper bound and a hint, not a hard promise — the driver and hardware are free to fetch less. And it is coarse: on most dense workloads you should leave it at the default. It earns its keep only when profiling shows a large gap between the bytes you logically touched and the DRAM bytes the memory system actually moved.

The diagnostic that sends you here is exactly that gap. If Nsight Compute reports you requested, say, 2 GB of logical loads but DRAM moved 6 GB, you are over-fetching — every scattered 32 B access is dragging a full 128 B line behind it — and dropping the granularity limit recovers the wasted 2/3. It is the HBM-side mirror of the sector-efficiency story we told for the L2-to-SM path.

Where this lands us

The L2 is not a knob you tune first — in the FP32 GEMM ladder it was never the bottleneck; those kernels sat at low single-digit percentages of L2 utilization while they fought over coalescing and shared-memory tiling, and even at 93.7% of cuBLAS the constraint was compute and SMEM scheduling, not L2 capacity. But the L2 is the structure that explains those other numbers: sector granularity is why coalescing matters, the crossbar is why atomics have the latency they do, and residency control is the escape hatch when a genuinely memory-bound kernel has a working set that would fit on-chip if only the cache would hold still.

Keep the mental model small. The L2 is one ~50 MiB SRAM shared by every SM, split into two partitions across a crossbar (near beats far), addressed in 128 B lines of four 32 B sectors (you are billed per sector), with compression as an occasional free tailwind and two real levers — cudaLimitPersistingL2CacheSize for what to keep and cudaLimitMaxL2FetchGranularity for how much to fetch. Next we drop one level further out and look at HBM itself — the 3.35 TB/s wall that the L2 spends all its time trying to keep you from hitting.
