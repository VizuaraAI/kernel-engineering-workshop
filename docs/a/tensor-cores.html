01 · The GPU, From Silicon Up

Tensor cores TC

When you read that an H100 does 989 TFLOP/s of BF16, it is easy to picture that number spread thinly across the whole chip — thousands of little cores each chipping in. It isn't. The overwhelming majority of that headline throughput — roughly 95% — comes from a small, specialized unit that does exactly one thing: it multiplies a tiny matrix by another tiny matrix and adds a third. Everything else on the SM — the schedulers, the FFMA pipes, the load/store units — exists to keep those units fed. If you want to understand why fast GPU kernels are shaped the way they are, you have to understand the Tensor Core first, because in the modern regime the kernel is not written with tensor cores, it is written around them.

This article is the last stop in the hardware tour before we start climbing the GEMM ladder. I want to make one thing concrete: what a tensor core actually computes, in what shapes, at what precision, and why that shape dictates the entire structure of a good matmul.

One instruction: D = A·B + C

A CUDA Core does a scalar fused-multiply-add: d = a * b + c, one number in each slot, one result out. That is the primitive the whole first half of this course is built on. A tensor core does the same operation one dimension up: it computes

D = A · B + C

where A, B, C, and D are small matrices, not scalars. This is a Matrix Multiply-Accumulate (MMA), and the accumulate half is not decorative — it is the entire point. A single MMA rarely computes a full output tile; it computes a slice of one and adds it onto a running accumulator that lives in registers, so that a sequence of MMAs marches along the k dimension of a big GEMM, each one folding another k-slice into the same C.1 This is why the C in D = A·B + C is almost always the same registers as D — you accumulate in place. The general library form is D = αA·B + βC, but inside the inner loop α and β are 1 and the fragment just keeps summing.

The shapes are fixed and small, baked into the silicon. The canonical Hopper/Ampere shape is written m16n8k16: the A fragment is 16 × 16, the B fragment is 16 × 8, and the C/D accumulator is 16 × 8. One such instruction performs 16 × 8 × 16 = 2048 multiply-accumulate operations. You will also see m16n8k8 (half the k) and the older Volta wmma shapes like m16n16k16; the exact legal set depends on precision and architecture, and nvcc rejects an illegal combination at PTX-assembly time. That is the fundamental grain of modern GPU compute: not one flop, but two thousand of them, issued as a single instruction.

Why ~95% of the FLOPs live here

There are only four tensor cores per SM on an H100 — one per warp scheduler — and with ~132 SMs that is a little over five hundred of them on the whole die. They are physically big and few, "much larger and less numerous than CUDA Cores" — the opposite of the many-tiny-cores mental model. But because each one retires 2048 MACs per issue, the arithmetic density is enormous. A CUDA core doing scalar FFMA retires one MAC per lane per cycle; a tensor core doing an MMA retires thousands. That is the entire source of the roughly two-orders-of-magnitude gap — NVIDIA quotes tensor cores at "100× more floating point operations per second than CUDA Cores."

Put numbers on it. The H100's FP32 CUDA-core throughput is on the order of 60 TFLOP/s. Its BF16 tensor-core throughput is 989 TFLOP/s. If you write a matmul that never touches a tensor core, the best case in the universe — perfect coalescing, perfect occupancy, zero memory stalls — caps out around that 60, which is why every kernel in the second half of this course, no matter how beautifully tuned, hits a ceiling far below cuBLAS. The library is on the tensor-core path; the scalar kernel is not. From the three regimes view, the tensor core is what makes a big GEMM compute-bound in the first place: it raises the ceiling so high that the memory system becomes the thing you fight.

The catch: fragments and warp cooperation

Here is the part that reshapes how you write code. A tensor core instruction is not a thread-level instruction — it is a warp-level one. All 32 threads of the warp must execute the MMA together, synchronously; if fewer than 32 arrive, the behavior is undefined. The mma.sync in the instruction name is that requirement made literal: sync, the whole warp, or nothing.

And the operands do not live in one place. The 16 × 16 tile of A that goes into one MMA is not held by one thread — it is smeared across all 32 threads of the warp, a few elements per thread, in a specific, non-obvious layout the hardware demands. These per-thread slices are called fragments. Each thread holds a handful of registers of A, a handful of B, and a handful of the C/D accumulator; the tensor core reads all of those registers at once, does the matrix multiply, and writes the accumulator fragments back. With 2048 MACs spread over 32 threads, that is exactly 2048 / 32 = 64 MACs contributed per thread per instruction.

This is the whole reason tensor-core kernels look strange the first time you read them. You cannot just load a tile into shared memory and start multiplying. You have to get the data into exactly the register layout the MMA expects, across all 32 lanes, before you can issue it. Doing that by hand with ordinary loads is miserable and slow, so there is a dedicated instruction for it: ldmatrix, which loads a rectangular tile from shared memory and shuffles the elements across the warp into fragment layout in one shot — optionally transposing B on the way in.2 ldmatrix is why shared-memory layout and bank conflicts matter so much for tensor-core GEMMs specifically. The MMA wants a fixed register layout; ldmatrix produces it; but it reads SMEM in a pattern that will conflict badly if you laid your tile out naively. A whole class of tensor-core bugs is really ldmatrix bank-conflict bugs.

The three ways to say it: wmma, mma.sync, wgmma

There is a ladder of abstraction for issuing these instructions, and picking the right rung is itself a performance decision.

  • wmma — the CUDA C++ nvcuda::wmma API, with load_matrix_sync, mma_sync, store_matrix_sync. It is portable and readable, and it hides the fragment layout entirely. It is also where you start — and where you leave performance on the table, because it makes conservative choices about loads and layouts you cannot override.3 The rule of thumb from every fast-matmul writeup: wmma gets you maybe 70–80% of what's possible with an order of magnitude less code; the last 20% requires dropping to PTX and controlling the loads yourself.
  • mma.sync — the PTX instruction itself, e.g. mma.sync.aligned.m16n8k16.row.col.f32.f16.f16.f32 (the type suffixes run in D A B C order — FP32 accumulate, FP16 inputs, FP32 accumulate). You manage the fragments, you place the ldmatrix calls, you own the register layout. This is what the fast open-source kernels and the middle of cuBLAS's history are built on: maximum control, per-warp, Ampere-style.
  • wgmma — Warp-Group MMA, new in Hopper (sm_90a). This is the modern path and it changes the unit of cooperation: instead of one warp (32 threads), a warp group of four warps (128 threads) issues one enormous asynchronous MMA together, and — crucially — it can read operand A straight from shared memory rather than requiring everything in registers first.4 wgmma is also asynchronous: you launch it, it runs on the tensor core, and you sync later. That async property is what lets Hopper kernels overlap the MMA with the next tile's TMA copy — the software-pipelining trick that gets cuBLAS past 90% of peak. Blackwell pushes this further with tcgen05 and a dedicated Tensor Memory (TMEM); that is a later article. It exists precisely because the register-file pressure of feeding a chip this fast through per-warp mma.sync had become the bottleneck. On Hopper, near-peak GEMM is a wgmma story.

The pattern across all three rungs is the same: describe a small tiled matmul, hand it to the hardware in the exact layout demanded, accumulate along k. The rungs differ only in who does the bookkeeping and how big the cooperating group is.

Precisions: the tensor core is a precision menu

The other thing the tensor core exposes is a choice of input precision, and it is a direct throughput knob. The accumulator is almost always FP32 (you want the sum to stay accurate), but the A and B inputs can be:

  • TF32 — a 19-bit format (FP32's 8-bit exponent, 10-bit mantissa) that lets FP32-ish training run on tensor cores at several times the true-FP32 (CUDA-core) rate. It is the "free" upgrade for models that were written in FP32.
  • FP16 / BF16 — the workhorses. BF16 keeps FP32's exponent range (great for training stability) with a 7-bit mantissa; this is the 989 TFLOP/s path. FP16 runs at the same rate with more mantissa but less range.
  • FP8 (e4m3 / e5m2) — Hopper's tensor cores double the rate again to roughly 2× BF16, at the cost of precision you must manage with scaling. This is the inference-and-increasingly-training frontier.5 Blackwell adds NVFP4 — a 4-bit e2m1 element with FP8 block scales — for another doubling. Different silicon, later course; the mental model (narrower inputs → more throughput → more scaling headaches) is identical.

Why the whole kernel is shaped around this

Step back and the design pressure becomes obvious. The tensor core is fast enough that on an H100 it can chew through a 16 × 8 × 16 tile in a couple of cycles, but HBM3 delivers "only" 3.35 TB/s. Do the roofline arithmetic from the three regimes and the ridge point sits near 295 FLOPs per byte — meaning to keep these units busy you must reuse every byte you load hundreds of times. That single fact dictates the entire architecture of a fast GEMM:

  1. Tile in shared memory and registers, because the tensor core will starve otherwise — the whole shared-memory and register-blocking story exists to feed the MMA.
  2. Get the fragment layout exactly right with ldmatrix, because a mislaid tile means bank conflicts and stalled MMAs.
  3. Overlap copy with compute — TMA loading the next tile while wgmma grinds the current one — because the async tensor core makes idle silicon the only remaining enemy.

None of that is about doing the math faster. The math is the fast part. Every technique in the rest of this course is about arranging bytes so the tensor core never waits. On the pure-CUDA-core ladder we are about to climb, we will hit 93.7% of cuBLAS and stall there — and the reason we stall is precisely that cuBLAS is on the tensor-core path and our kernel is not. When we finally cross over to wmma, then mma.sync, and finally wgmma, the ceiling jumps by the better part of an order of magnitude, and we spend the rest of our time not computing faster but feeding the beast without a single wasted byte.
