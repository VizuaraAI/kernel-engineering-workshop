A weight matrix in a large language model is, at inference time, dead weight. You read it, you multiply by it once per token, you never write it back. On the [memory-bound decode path](prefill-vs-decode.html) — one token at a time, no reuse — the entire cost of a linear layer is the cost of streaming its weights out of HBM. So the single most effective thing you can do to a decode kernel is not make the math faster; it is make the weights *smaller*. Store them in four bits instead of sixteen and you move a quarter of the bytes, and on a bandwidth-bound kernel fewer bytes is the whole game.

That is the promise of quantization, and it is also the trap. The bytes get smaller for free; making the *kernel* faster is not free at all. This article is about the kernels that turn a compressed weight format into a matmul — how you store the scales, how you unpack four-bit integers inside the inner loop, why **W4A16** is the workhorse of LLM serving, and where FP8 fits. The throughline, as everywhere on this site, is the [three regimes](the-three-regimes.html): quantization is a bytes-movement trick, so we will judge every kernel by whether the decode disappears into the load.

## The real win is memory traffic, not math

Start with the arithmetic, because it is the argument. A 70B-parameter weight in BF16 is 140 GB. Serving one token means streaming all of it past the arithmetic units once. On an H100 at roughly **3.35 TB/s** of HBM bandwidth, 140 GB is a hard floor of about **42 ms per token** just to read the weights — before you do a single useful flop.[[sn: This is the decode reality: with batch size 1 there is essentially no weight reuse, so the linear layers are pure bandwidth. The [batched-decode](batched-decode-matvec.html) article is about clawing some reuse back by processing many sequences at once; quantization is the orthogonal lever that shrinks the bytes themselves.]] Halve the precision to FP8 and that floor halves to ~21 ms; quarter it to 4-bit weights and it drops toward ~10.5 ms. The token rate roughly *inverts* the bytes-per-weight. That is why quantization is the highest-leverage inference optimization after fusion: it attacks the exact resource — HBM bandwidth — that decode is bound on.

Crucially, the win is memory traffic, not fewer flops. In **W4A16** you dequantize the 4-bit weights back up to 16-bit *inside the kernel* and do the matmul in 16-bit. You perform the *same* number of multiply-adds you would have in BF16; you just fed them from a quarter of the bytes. The compute is unchanged. The load is quartered. On a memory-bound kernel that is the entire speedup, and it is why "does it reduce flops?" is the wrong question to ask of a quantized decode kernel.

There is a price, and it is accuracy. Four bits can represent only sixteen distinct levels; squeezing a weight distribution that spans several orders of magnitude into sixteen levels throws away information. Done naively — one scale for a whole matrix — the quantization error is large enough to visibly degrade a model. The craft of quantization is spending your scale budget so that the error stays small, and that is where the kernel's data layout comes from.

## Scales: per-tensor, per-channel, per-group

A quantized weight is not just an integer. It is an integer *plus a scale*: the real value is reconstructed as `w ≈ q × scale` (and for asymmetric schemes, `w ≈ (q − zero_point) × scale`). The only question is how many weights share one scale, and that single choice trades memory against accuracy.

- **Per-tensor**: one `scale` for the entire matrix. Cheapest to store — a single float — and cheapest in the kernel, because the scale is a constant you can hoist out of the inner loop. But one scale must cover every weight, so a few large outliers stretch the range and everything else quantizes coarsely. Accuracy suffers.
- **Per-channel**: one scale per output channel (per column of the weight matrix). Now each row of the output has its own scale, so a noisy channel no longer poisons its neighbors. This is the standard choice for weight-only quantization: the scale vector is tiny (one float per output feature) and it folds naturally into the accumulator at the end of each dot product.[[sn: Per-*channel* along the output dimension is cheap because the scale is constant across the whole `K`-contraction for a given output — you accumulate the integer dot product, then multiply by the one channel scale once. Per-channel along the *contraction* dimension is the expensive case and is usually done as grouping instead.]]
- **Per-group (block)**: split the contraction dimension `K` into small groups — commonly **128**, or as small as **16** in microscaling formats — and give each group its own scale. This is what modern 4-bit schemes use, because it keeps a tight range around each little block of weights. The cost is that the kernel must fetch and apply a *new* scale every group as it marches down `K`, which puts the scale application squarely inside the inner loop.

The finer the granularity, the better the accuracy and the more scales you move. Per-group at group size 128 adds one FP16 scale per 128 weights — under 1% overhead on the bytes — for a large accuracy recovery, which is why it dominates. The [NVFP4 microscaling](nvfp4-microscaling.html) format pushes this to the extreme: a group of 16 with an FP8 scale each.

## Dequantize in the kernel: the W4A16 inner loop

Here is the hypothesis that makes W4A16 fast: **never let the 4-bit weights exist in memory as anything but 4-bit.** If you dequantize the whole matrix to FP16 in a separate pass and write it back to HBM, you have thrown away the entire benefit — you just moved 16-bit bytes again. The dequantize has to happen *inside* the matmul kernel, on data that is already in registers, so that HBM only ever sees the packed 4-bit form.

So the kernel loads packed `int4` weights (two per byte), unpacks and dequantizes them to FP16 in registers, and feeds the FP16 activations and the just-dequantized FP16 weights into the multiply-accumulate. The activations stay 16-bit throughout — that is the "A16" — so accumulation precision and range are exactly what a normal FP16 matmul would have. Sketching the inner loop:

```cpp
// W4A16 inner loop over one K-group (group size G, e.g. 128).
half  s   = scales[out_ch][k_group];       // per-group FP16 scale, hoisted
for (int k = 0; k < G; k += 2) {
    uint8_t packed = qweight[k >> 1];      // two 4-bit weights in one byte
    int q_lo =  packed        & 0x0F;      // low nibble
    int q_hi = (packed >> 4)  & 0x0F;      // high nibble
    half w_lo = __int2half_rn(q_lo - 8) * s;   // dequantize (zero-point 8)
    half w_hi = __int2half_rn(q_hi - 8) * s;
    acc = __hfma(a[k],   w_lo, acc);       // 16-bit activation × 16-bit weight
    acc = __hfma(a[k+1], w_hi, acc);
}
```

The `- 8` is the symmetric zero-point for a signed 4-bit range mapped from `[0,15]` to `[-8,7]`; the scale is applied per group. The load side moves one byte per two weights; the compute side is ordinary FP16 FMA. This is the shape of every production W4A16 kernel — the details differ (vectorized nibble unpacking, dequantizing eight weights at once with SIMD-in-a-register tricks, prefetching the next group's scale) but the skeleton is *load packed → unpack → scale → FMA*, and the packed weights never leave 4-bit until they are in a register.

The subtle part is that unpacking is not free. Every `& 0x0F`, every shift, every `__int2half_rn` is a real integer instruction, and there are as many of them as there are weights. On a well-tuned W4A16 kernel this is fine — the load was the bottleneck and there is plenty of idle ALU to hide the unpack behind. But push the format low enough and the *decode itself* becomes the bottleneck, which is exactly the wall the [NVFP4 worklog](nvfp4-microscaling.html) hits: a 4-bit GEMV where hand-rolled bit-twiddling issued roughly **10× more instructions** than necessary, and the fix was not moving fewer bytes but replacing the unpack with a hardware conversion intrinsic (`__nv_cvt_fp4x2_to_halfraw2`), which collapsed the kernel from 443 µs to 39 µs. The lesson generalizes: *at low enough precision, the dequantize is the kernel*, and you want the silicon's `cvt` unit, not clever masking.[[sn: The rule of thumb: W4A16 with group-128 on Ampere/Hopper is comfortably load-bound and the integer unpack hides fine. Below that — genuine 4-bit floats, group-16 microscaling — the per-value decode cost catches up to the byte savings, and only hardware conversion units keep the decode free. That is precisely why Blackwell added native FP4 conversion and MMA paths.]]

## FP8: e4m3 vs e5m2, and W8A8

Eight bits is the other rung, and it is a floating-point format rather than an integer one, which changes the tradeoffs. FP8 comes in two flavors that differ only in how they split their bits between exponent and mantissa:

- **e4m3** — 1 sign, **4 exponent**, **3 mantissa** bits. More mantissa means more precision but less range (it saturates around ±448). This is the format for **weights and activations** in the forward pass, where you want precision and the values are reasonably bounded.
- **e5m2** — 1 sign, **5 exponent**, **2 mantissa** bits. More exponent means wider dynamic range (out to ±57344) but coarser precision. It is the format for **gradients** in training, where the values span a huge range and you care more about not overflowing than about the last bit of precision.[[sn: The e4m3 / e5m2 split is the same "precision vs range" dial as FP16 vs BF16, just at eight bits. Inference kernels almost always mean e4m3 when they say "FP8"; e5m2 shows up in the training backward pass. Both are IEEE-ish but with their own special-value conventions, so the decode intrinsic you pick has to name the exact format.]]

For inference, FP8 usually means **W8A8**: both weights *and* activations are 8-bit, so — unlike W4A16 — the tensor cores can consume the low-precision operands *directly*. Hopper and Blackwell have FP8 tensor-core MMA paths, so a W8A8 GEMM does not dequantize to 16-bit at all; it multiplies FP8 × FP8 and accumulates in FP32. That is a genuine compute win on top of the bandwidth win — roughly 2× the tensor-core throughput of FP16 — which is why FP8 matters for the *compute-bound* prefill as well as memory-bound decode, whereas weight-only W4A16 helps mostly decode.

This is the split worth remembering. **W4A16** trades a per-value dequantize (integer unpack in the kernel) for maximum bandwidth savings on memory-bound decode; the tensor cores still run at 16-bit. **W8A8/FP8** keeps everything 8-bit end to end so the tensor cores run natively at low precision, buying both bandwidth and compute — the reason frontier models reach for it. DeepSeek's V4-Pro-DSpark makes exactly this call in production: **FP8 for most parameters and the KV cache, FP4 for the MoE expert weights**,[[sn: From the DeepSeek-V4-Pro-DSpark model card: MoE expert parameters in FP4, most other parameters in FP8, and `--kv-cache-dtype fp8` in the serving config. The experts are the parameter bulk, so pushing *them* to 4 bits is where the memory win concentrates, while the more sensitive dense and attention weights stay at the safer 8-bit rung.]] concentrating the aggressive 4-bit quantization exactly where the parameters are heaviest and the bandwidth win is largest.

## The kernel structure, and the accuracy tradeoff

Zoom out and every quantized matmul has the same three-part structure, and the design work is deciding where the boundaries fall:

1. **Load** the packed low-precision weights and their scales from HBM. This is the step you are optimizing *for* — the whole point was to make it small. Coalesce it, and prefetch the next group's scale so the scale traffic hides behind the weight traffic.
2. **Decode** — unpack nibbles or convert FP8, apply the per-group scale, land in whatever precision the math unit wants (16-bit for W4A16, or straight into an FP8 tensor-core op for W8A8). This step must stay cheap; when it doesn't, you reach for a hardware `cvt` intrinsic rather than hand-written bit manipulation.
3. **Accumulate** — the actual multiply-adds, in FP16 or FP32. Unchanged from a normal matmul. This is why quantization does not touch your flop count.

And hanging over all of it is the accuracy tradeoff, which is not a kernel concern but decides which kernel you are allowed to ship. Fewer bits means coarser values means more quantization error; the counterweights are finer scale granularity (per-group over per-tensor) and keeping the sensitive parts of the network at higher precision (DeepSeek's dense-in-FP8, experts-in-FP4 split is exactly this instinct). The engineering equilibrium is: push the *bulk* of the parameters to the lowest precision that holds accuracy, spend a little bandwidth on per-group scales to hold the range, and let the kernel dequantize on the fly so HBM only ever sees the compressed form.

That is the whole discipline. Quantization is not a math optimization dressed as a memory one — it is a memory optimization, full stop, and it works for exactly the reason the [three regimes](the-three-regimes.html) predict: on the decode path you are bound by the bytes you drag out of HBM, and the fastest way to move fewer bytes is to store fewer bits per weight. Everything else — the scales, the nibble unpacking, the FP8 conversion intrinsics, the accuracy tuning — is in service of making that byte reduction real without the decode eating the win.
