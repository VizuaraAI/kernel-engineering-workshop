Every kernel in the [GEMM ladder](gemm-kernel-1-naive.html) I climbed by hand was a search — I just happened to be the search algorithm. State a hypothesis, write the smallest kernel that tests it, profile it, read the bottleneck the profiler hands back, pick the next move. That loop has a shape, and the shape is a program search: propose a candidate, compile it, profile it, keep the good ones, mutate them, repeat. The obvious question, once you've done it a dozen times by hand, is whether a language model can be dropped into that loop instead of me. It turns out the answer is a resounding *yes* — and the interesting part is not that it works, but *how cheaply* it works if you stop asking the model to be smart and start asking it to be plural.

This article is about test-time scaling for kernel generation: what happens when you stop drawing one sample from a model and start drawing a hundred, and then when you wrap that sampling in an evolutionary loop with an archive of everything that worked so far. I'll walk the numbers from two 2025 lines of work — the Anne Ouyang / Stanford CRFM effort behind KernelBench, and the parallel evolutionary-search results from Sakana AI and DeepMind — and try to explain why the *search* framing, not the *model* framing, is the one that predicts the results.[[sn: Primary sources for the numbers here: Simon Guo's survey *"Automated GPU Kernels"* (2025-10) and the Stanford CRFM post *"Surprisingly Fast AI-Generated Kernels"* (2025-05-28). Where I quote a percentage, it comes from one of those.]]

## The benchmark: KernelBench, and what "fast_p" means

You cannot search without a fitness function, so start there. **KernelBench** is the benchmark everyone in this corner of the field reports against. It hands the model a PyTorch reference module and asks for a custom CUDA kernel that (a) produces numerically-matching output and (b) runs faster. It has three levels: **Level 1** is single operators (a matmul, a conv), **Level 2** is short *fused* operator sequences — a conv followed by a bias followed by a ReLU, the kind of thing where fusion is the whole game — and **Level 3** is end-to-end architectures like a ViT or a Mamba block.

The metric is `fast_p`: the fraction of problems for which the model produced a kernel that is both **correct** and at least `p` times faster than the PyTorch baseline. Correctness is not a proof — it's numerical equality against the reference over many random inputs, at a tolerance of `1e-02`.[[sn: That `1e-02` tolerance is generous, and deliberately so: a fused kernel reorders floating-point accumulation, so bit-exact output is impossible. The looseness is also a small attack surface — a kernel can technically "pass" while being subtly wrong on inputs the random sampler never hit. Every serious harness re-checks the winners.]] So the reward signal a search loop optimizes against is a two-stage gate: first *does it compile and match*, then *how much faster is it*. Both stages are cheap, mechanical, and require no human — which is exactly the property you need to turn generation into search.

## Monkeys: the embarrassing power of drawing many samples

Here is the result that reframed the whole area. Take **DeepSeek-V3**, a model with no special kernel training, and ask it for a Level 2 kernel *once*. It succeeds — correct and faster — about **4%** of the time. That is roughly what you'd expect: writing a correct, fused, faster-than-PyTorch CUDA kernel in one shot is hard, and a general model mostly fails.

Now draw **100 independent samples** per problem, compile and check every one, and keep the best. The `fast_1` success rate jumps from **4% to 37%**. Same model, same prompt, no feedback, no cleverness — just a hundred rolls of the dice against a mechanical verifier. The verifier is what makes this legitimate: you are not hoping the model is right, you are *filtering* a hundred guesses through a gate that cannot be fooled about compilation and can barely be fooled about correctness. This is the "infinite monkeys" phenomenon — repeated sampling with a checker turns a weak generator into a strong solver, because the checker, not the generator, is carrying the reliability.[[sn: The "monkeys" name comes from the *Large Language Monkeys* line of work (Brown et al.), which showed coverage — the chance *any* of `k` samples is correct — climbing smoothly, often log-linearly, with `k` across coding and math. Kernels are an unusually clean case because the checker is a compiler plus a numerical diff.]]

Why does this work so much better than it has any right to? Because `pass@k` is governed by *coverage*, not by the mean sample quality. If a single sample solves a problem with probability `p`, the chance that at least one of `k` samples solves it is `1 − (1−p)^k`. Plug in a dismal `p = 0.04` and `k = 100`: `1 − 0.96^100 ≈ 0.98`. The math says a 4%-per-try generator should clear nearly every problem in 100 tries — the gap between that ceiling and the measured 37% is precisely the problems where the model's `p` is *zero*, where no amount of resampling helps because the model simply cannot express the required idea. Sampling buys you coverage over the ideas the model *can* reach; it buys nothing over the ones it can't.

## Serial feedback: turning the profiler into the prompt

Parallel sampling is memoryless — a hundred blind guesses that never learn from each other. The second axis is *serial* refinement: run one kernel, feed its compiler errors and its `ncu` profile back into the next prompt, and let the model react to evidence the way a human worklog does. When the Stanford group did this with **DeepSeek-R1** — iterative turns carrying verifier and profiler feedback — Level 2 climbed from a single-shot **36%** to about **72%**.[[sn: The `72%` figure is DeepSeek-R1's, and its single-shot baseline is `36%` — a different model from the DeepSeek-V3 whose 100-sample number was `37%`. The two lines happen to start near the same place, which is a coincidence of the benchmark, not one run continuing into the other. R1 is a reasoning model, so it benefits from the profiler feedback in a way a base model does not.]]

That number is worth sitting with. A general reasoning model, given a profiler and a few turns, correctly and faster-than-PyTorch solves roughly **72%** of the fusion problems on Level 2 — a clean doubling from its own single-shot rate. The feedback is doing something the parallel samples could not: it moves the model's *conditional* distribution. A profile that says "you are memory-bound, occupancy is 25%, these loads are uncoalesced" is exactly the [three-regimes](the-three-regimes.html) diagnostic I'd read myself, and conditioning the next sample on it shifts probability mass toward the fix. Parallel sampling widens coverage; serial feedback deepens it. You want both, and the natural way to get both is to stop thinking in samples and start thinking in *search*.

## The propose → compile → profile → iterate loop, drawn as search

Both knobs are really one thing: a search over the space of programs, where the moves are *generate a variant* and the fitness is *fast_p*. Once you name it that, the whole vocabulary of search becomes available — and the best 2025 systems reach for it.

The single most productive idea, from Ouyang et al., is to **split the move in two**: first have the model propose an *optimization idea in natural language* ("stage the B tile in shared memory", "vectorize the loads with `float4`", "pad to avoid bank conflicts"), and only then have it realize that idea as code. This is the same decomposition I use as a human — I decide *what* to try before I write *how* — and it matters for search because ideas are a far smaller, far more diverse space than raw code. You can enumerate five genuinely different strategies at a node; you cannot easily enumerate five genuinely different 200-line kernels, because greedy code-generation collapses toward the same local shape every time.

The CRFM group pushed this into a **branching search** rather than a linear chain of revisions. Each idea fans out into multiple implementations; the fastest kernels of a round *seed* the next round; they ran 5 rounds with OpenAI `o3` and Gemini 2.5 Pro over 10 KernelBench Level 1 problems, and — tellingly — most of the winning kernels appeared only in **rounds 4 and 5**. The good stuff was several mutations deep. A greedy loop that kept only the single best child each round would have pruned the lineage that led to it before it ever got fast. Branching keeps the tree alive long enough for a late idea to compound on an earlier one.

Here is the loop as pseudocode, because the shape is the point:

```python
archive = []                          # every correct kernel we have ever found
seeds   = [pytorch_reference]

for round in range(NUM_ROUNDS):
    children = []
    for seed in top_k(seeds, K):      # branch from the current best
        ideas = model.propose_ideas(seed, profile_of(seed))   # NL, diverse
        for idea in ideas:            # fan out: one idea -> many impls
            for _ in range(SAMPLES_PER_IDEA):
                kernel = model.write_code(seed, idea)
                if not compiles(kernel):            continue   # gate 1
                if not correct(kernel, tol=1e-2):   continue   # gate 2
                kernel.speed = benchmark(kernel)               # fitness
                children.append(kernel)
                archive.append(kernel)
    seeds = children                  # survivors seed the next round

best = max(archive, key=lambda k: k.speed)
```

The three roles map cleanly onto the discipline of a hand-written worklog. `propose_ideas` is the *hypothesis*. `write_code` plus the two gates is *implement and verify correctness*. `benchmark` and `profile_of` are *profile and read the bottleneck*. The archive is the thing a solo engineer keeps only in their head: a memory of everything that ever worked, so a dead-end round can restart from a known-good ancestor instead of from scratch.

## The archive: evolution, not just sampling

That archive is what turns "best-of-N sampling" into genuine **evolutionary search**, and it is the through-line of the strongest systems. **Sakana AI's AI CUDA Engineer** maintains an active archive of good kernels discovered during exploration and evolves against it — crossing over and mutating past winners rather than always regenerating from the reference. **DeepMind's AlphaEvolve** wraps the same idea in an automated-verifier + evolutionary-framework loop and reports a **32.5%** speedup on a JAX/Pallas FlashAttention kernel — a real kernel, in a real framework, found by a machine running the propose-verify-keep loop at scale.

The measured ceiling of this loop is genuinely startling. On an L40S, the CRFM branching search produced kernels beating stock PyTorch on real ops: **101.3%** of `torch.matmul` on a 4096² FP32 matmul, **179.9%** of `torch.nn.Conv2D`, and — the headline — **484.4%** of `torch.nn.LayerNorm`.[[sn: Those percentages are on an L40S, not the H100 this course otherwise targets, and they are ratios of *reference time to kernel time*, so >100% means faster than the baseline. Read them as "a search loop can beat a naive framework op by multiples", not "AI beats cuBLAS" — the LayerNorm baseline is a soft target, and none of these approach a hand-tuned `wgmma` GEMM.]] One Conv2D trajectory ran **13 rounds**, dragging a kernel from `7.02 ms` (20.1% of reference speed) to `0.795 ms` (179.9%) — a 9× self-improvement, entirely inside the loop.

## What the search can and cannot reach

The honest caveat is the one the coverage math already gave us: search finds ideas the model can *express*, and nothing beyond. Level 1 and 2 — single ops and short fusions — are where these numbers live, because the winning moves are shared memory, coalescing, vectorized `float4` loads, tiling: exactly the vocabulary the model has seen ten thousand times and exactly the ladder I climbed by hand. The frontier tricks in this course — Hopper's `wgmma`, `TMA`-driven async pipelines, thread-block clusters staging through 228 KiB of shared memory — are far rarer in the training data, and the search correspondingly struggles to *propose* them, no matter how many samples you draw. You cannot resample your way to an idea with `p = 0`.

Which is, I think, the most useful way to hold this. Test-time scaling did not make the kernel engineer obsolete; it made the *verifier* the center of gravity. The reason a hundred blind samples beat one careful one is that KernelBench's compile-and-diff gate is trustworthy enough to sift them. The reason branching beats greedy is that the archive remembers what the gate approved. Everything I do by hand in the rest of this course — predict the regime, read the SASS, keep a log of what worked — is the same loop; the machine just runs it wider and never gets tired. The next article climbs into that loop from the inside: how to build the harness — the compile sandbox, the correctness diff, the profiler scrape — that makes a search over kernels safe to run a hundred thousand times.
