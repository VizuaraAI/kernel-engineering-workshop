Every generation of GPU buys speed by moving fewer bits. FP32 became FP16 became BF16 became FP8, and each halving roughly doubled the tensor-core throughput the silicon could offer for the same die area and the same power budget. Blackwell takes the next step and it is a strange one: **NVFP4**, a four-bit floating-point format where the individual numbers are so coarse that they cannot represent your data at all on their own. The trick — the entire trick — is that they don't have to. NVFP4 is a *microscaling* format, and understanding what that means is the difference between a kernel that runs at 2000 microseconds and one that runs at 22.3.

This article is the frontier of the ladder. Everywhere else on this site we have been closing the gap to `cuBLAS` in a fixed precision; here the precision itself is the optimization, and the kernel's job is to unpack a compressed number format fast enough that the tensor cores never wait. I am going to walk through a real hackathon worklog — an NVFP4 GEMV on Blackwell that started at 2000 µs and finished at **22.3 µs**, a 90× climb[[sn: The worklog this article rebuilds is Yue Zhang's Blackwell NVFP4 kernel hackathon journey (2025). The numbers, the intrinsics, and the ordering of the wins are all from that writeup; the voice and the figures are ours.]] — because the shape of that climb tells you exactly what a four-bit kernel is actually bound on.

## What NVFP4 actually is

A single NVFP4 value is **e2m1**: one sign bit, two exponent bits, one mantissa bit. Four bits total. That gives you a grand menu of about sixteen representable values, spaced logarithmically, topping out around 6 in magnitude. On its own this is useless for a neural network — you cannot store a weight distribution that spans several orders of magnitude in a format whose largest number is 6.

The rescue is **microscaling** (MX): you don't store one scale for the whole tensor, you store one scale for every small *block* of values. In NVFP4 the block is 16 elements, and each block carries its own **FP8** scale factor in the `e4m3` layout[[sn: The reference kernel's scale factors are stored `e4m3fnuz` — the "fnuz" variant has no infinities and a single (negative) zero, freeing an extra exponent code. The exact FP8 flavor matters when you pick the decode intrinsic; get it wrong and your dequantized values are silently off.]]. So the real number you reconstruct is `fp4_value × fp8_block_scale`. The four bits carry the *shape* of each number within its block; the eight-bit scale carries the *magnitude* of the block. Sixteen coarse values riding on one shared, finer exponent — that is the whole idea.

The storage math is worth internalising. Sixteen values at 4 bits is 8 bytes, plus one FP8 scale is 1 more byte, for 9 bytes per 16 elements — about **4.5 effective bits per element**. Compared to BF16's 16 bits, you are moving roughly 3.5× fewer bytes. On a **bandwidth-bound** kernel like a GEMV — where you stream a huge matrix past a tiny vector and the [three regimes](the-three-regimes.html) verdict is memory, memory, memory — that byte reduction *is* the speedup, if and only if the decode is free. Making the decode free is the entire worklog.

## The workload and the naive start

The problem is a batched GEMV: a matrix `A` and a matrix `B`, both stored `M × K × L` in K-major order as NVFP4, with scale tensors `sfa` and `sfb` shaped `M × (K/16) × L` in FP8. One scale per 16-element block along `K`. Contract over `K`, keep the batch `L`. This is memory-bound by construction — there is almost no reuse, you read each packed byte essentially once and do a couple of flops with it.

The naive kernel does the obvious thing and pays for it. It reads packed `uint8` bytes, and for every four-bit value it reconstructs the float by hand: mask off the sign bit, shift out the two exponent bits, pull the mantissa bit, reassemble an FP16 bit pattern, then do the same dance for the FP8 scale, multiply, accumulate. It is correct. It runs at **2000 µs**. Every one of those handwritten shifts and masks is a real instruction, and there are billions of them.

The first, dullest win is the one from the rest of this site: fix the access pattern. Coalesce the global loads so a warp reads contiguous packed bytes, and let 32 threads collaborate on one output row with a shared-memory partial-sum reduction instead of one thread grinding a whole dot product alone[[sn: Same warp-collaboration idea as a reduction kernel — 32 lanes each accumulate a slice of the `K` dimension, then a shuffle-based tree sum combines them. It is orthogonal to the decode work but has to be in place first, or the decode optimizations have nothing to feed.]]. That alone drops us from 2000 µs to **443 µs**. Good, but still nowhere. The profiler now points at something specific and slightly humiliating.

## The intrinsics win: stop twiddling bits

Here is the hypothesis that reshapes everything. I profiled the CUDA kernel next to a reference CuTe-DSL version of the same math, and the CUDA code was issuing roughly **10× more instructions** for the same work. That is not a memory problem anymore; that is an *instruction-count* problem hiding inside a memory-bound kernel. All those handwritten masks and shifts to decode e2m1 and e4m3 were compiling to long sequences of integer ALU ops, and the warp was spending its time decoding, not loading.

The fix is that Blackwell has hardware that does this decode in a single instruction. There are dedicated conversion intrinsics that take packed low-precision bytes straight to half-precision:

```cpp
// Decode two packed NVFP4 (e2m1) values -> two halfs, in one op.
__half2_raw two_fp4 = __nv_cvt_fp4x2_to_halfraw2(packed_fp4x2, __NV_E2M1);

// Decode one packed FP8 (e4m3) scale -> one half.
__half_raw scale    = __nv_cvt_fp8_to_halfraw(packed_fp8, __NV_E4M3);
```

`__nv_cvt_fp4x2_to_halfraw2` converts *two* NVFP4 values at once into an `__half2`, and `__nv_cvt_fp8_to_halfraw` handles the scale. What was a dozen integer instructions per value becomes one hardware conversion per pair. Replacing the hand-rolled bit-twiddling with these two intrinsics collapses the instruction count and drops the kernel from 443 µs to **39 µs** — an **11× improvement** from a change that made the code *shorter*.

## Squeezing the last order of magnitude

At 39 µs the decode is cheap, so the bottleneck moves again — now it is load latency and instruction scheduling. Three refinements take us the rest of the way, and each is a small, measured step.

**PTX for the fused multiply-accumulate.** The compiler was still generating separate conversion, scale-multiply, and accumulate instructions with sub-optimal packing. Dropping to inline PTX lets me fuse the whole per-block sequence into one hand-scheduled block: convert the packed pair with `cvt`, apply the block scale, and run the `fma.rn.f16x2` chain across the packed elements, keeping everything in `f16x2` so every op processes two values at once[[sn: The half-precision packed intrinsics operate on `f16x2` — a pair of halfs in one 32-bit register. Almost every op in the inner loop is a `.f16x2` variant precisely so the vector width is 2, which is why decoding to `__half2` in the intrinsic step lines up so neatly with the FMA step.]]. This trims 39 µs to about **27 µs**.

**Instruction-level parallelism over tiles.** A single tile per iteration means the FMA on this tile's data cannot start until this tile's load returns — the warp stalls on memory. So I processed **two tiles per iteration**, issuing tile 1's loads before consuming tile 0, so that by the time tile 0's math is done tile 1's bytes have arrived. This is software pipelining by hand, hiding the load latency behind the decode-and-FMA work, and it drops us to about **22.9 µs**.

```cpp
// ILP: overlap the load of the next tile with the compute of this one.
load(tile0);                 // A, B packed bytes + FP8 scales
load(tile1);                 // issued before tile0 is consumed -> overlaps
process(tile0);              // decode + scale + fma; tile1's bytes in flight
process(tile1);
```

I tried three and four tiles per iteration expecting more of the same and got the opposite: they were *slightly slower*. The reason is register pressure — more tiles in flight means more live half-registers, and past two tiles the extra pressure costs more in occupancy than it buys in latency hiding. Two was the sweet spot, and the final submission landed at **22.3 µs** — a **90× speedup** over the 2000 µs start, essentially all of it from making the four-bit decode disappear into hardware.

## Two roads up the mountain: CuTe-DSL vs raw CUDA

There is a second path worth naming, because it frames what these formats will feel like to write going forward. Alongside the raw-CUDA kernel I kept a **CuTe-DSL** version — CuTe being the CUDA Templates layout/tiling abstraction, here exposed through a Python-flavored domain-specific language. Starting from a template it began at about **100 µs** and, with a handful of refinements, reached roughly **33 µs**: reduce redundant scale-factor loads with selective indexing, store `A`/`B` in `float16` registers instead of `float32` to cut register pressure, collapse each block down to a single scale product, accumulate the raw elements first and apply that scale once at the end, and do the warp-collaborative partial-sum reduction through shared memory across 32 threads per row.

The honest comparison: the hand-tuned raw-CUDA path (22.3 µs) beat the CuTe-DSL path (33 µs) here — but the DSL got to a *respectable* number with far less code and far less bit-level fiddling, and I only outran it because I was willing to write PTX and hand-schedule tiles[[sn: The author's own caveat, which I'll keep: the CuTe-DSL skill was new — learned *during* the hackathon. A more fluent CuTe user might well close or invert that gap. The lesson is not "DSLs are slower"; it's "abstractions have a floor you hit fast and a ceiling you reach slowly."]]. That is the trade the whole frontier is built on: the DSL gives you a fast floor and a gentle slope; raw CUDA plus intrinsics plus PTX gives you the last 30%, at the cost of every optimization being yours to find.

## Why this is the frontier

Step back and the shape of the climb is the lesson. This kernel was memory-bound the whole time — a GEMV always is — and yet the single biggest win, the 11×, came from *reducing instructions*, not from moving fewer bytes. That is the signature of low-precision formats: the byte savings are handed to you by the format, but they are worthless unless the decode is free, and "free" means a hardware conversion unit, not clever code. Bit-twiddling a four-bit float by hand is a category error; the silicon has a `cvt` for it.

Microscaling is where numerics and kernel engineering fuse. NVFP4 is not just "smaller numbers" — it is a *layout* (16 values plus one scale), a *decode* (two intrinsics), and a *schedule* (pipeline the loads), and getting all three right is what turns 4.5 bits per element into a real 90× on real hardware. Blackwell adds more here — Tensor Memory, `tcgen05` MMA, CTA pairs — that let the tensor cores consume these formats natively at even larger tile granularity, and that is the next rung. But the habit is the same one from [the three regimes](the-three-regimes.html): predict the bound, measure it, and when the profiler says you are issuing 10× too many instructions, believe it, and go find the intrinsic that makes them disappear.
